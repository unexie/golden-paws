Golden Retriever Hot Spots: Causes, Treatment & Prevention

April 2026 · 8 min read · Golden Paws

Your Golden seemed perfectly fine at breakfast — and by dinnertime there's a red, oozing, painful-looking patch hidden under that beautiful coat. Welcome to the world of hot spots, one of the fastest and most dramatic skin problems your Golden Retriever can develop. The good news: hot spots are highly treatable, and once you understand why Goldens are so prone to them, prevention becomes much easier.

What Hot Spots Actually Are (and Why Goldens Get Them)

A hot spot — clinically known as acute moist dermatitis or pyotraumatic dermatitis — is a localized, rapidly spreading patch of inflamed, infected skin. It usually starts as a tiny irritation: a flea bite, a scratch, a damp spot under matted fur. Your dog licks or scratches at it, breaking the skin barrier. Within hours, bacteria multiply in that warm, moist micro-environment, and what was a mild itch becomes a red, weepy, painful lesion the size of a coaster.

Golden Retrievers are at the top of the list of hot-spot-prone breeds for three very specific reasons. First, that gorgeous double coat traps heat and moisture against the skin — exactly the conditions bacteria love. Second, Goldens have a documented genetic predisposition to atopic dermatitis (environmental allergies) and food sensitivities, which keep the skin in a chronically itchy, lower-threshold state. Third, Goldens love water — and a half-dried Golden after a swim is a hot spot waiting to happen.

Common hot spot locations on a Golden include the side of the face and neck (especially under the ears), the rump near the tail base, the hip and flank, and along the chest where the coat is thickest.

Spotting a Hot Spot: Early Warning Signs

Catching a hot spot in the first 12 hours can mean the difference between a simple at-home cleanup and a vet visit with antibiotics. Run your hands through your Golden's coat daily — feathering and the undercoat hide a lot. Watch for:

  • Sudden, focused licking or chewing at one spot, especially after a swim, bath, or long day outside.
  • A warm, damp area in the coat that doesn't quite smell right — slightly yeasty or sour.
  • Red, shiny skin when you part the fur, often surrounded by a darker, matted halo of saliva-stained hair.
  • A patch your dog flinches away from when touched. Hot spots are genuinely painful.
  • Hair loss in a clean circle as the lesion expands outward.

If your Golden suddenly becomes obsessive about one body part — even briefly — investigate immediately. A hot spot can double in size between morning and evening.

The Real Causes Behind Your Golden's Hot Spots

The lesion you see is the symptom; the real question is what triggered the licking in the first place. Veterinary dermatologists point to several common culprits in Goldens:

  • Flea allergy dermatitis — the single most common trigger. A Golden allergic to flea saliva can have a full-blown hot spot from one bite, even if you never see a flea.
  • Environmental allergies (atopy) — pollen, grass, dust mites. Goldens are statistically among the most atopy-prone breeds.
  • Food sensitivities — usually to common proteins like chicken or beef, manifesting as itchy paws, ears, and skin.
  • Trapped moisture — incomplete drying after swimming, bathing, or being out in rain.
  • Matted or compacted undercoat — especially behind ears, under the collar, and along the rump.
  • Ear infections — Goldens' floppy ears are notorious, and an itchy ear leads to scratching that creates hot spots on the side of the face.
  • Anal gland issues, hip pain, or even boredom — anything that makes your dog target one body part repeatedly.

Treating a Hot Spot at Home (and When to Call the Vet)

For a small, fresh hot spot — under about an inch across, not bleeding or oozing pus — you can usually start treatment at home. The protocol is simple but must be done thoroughly:

  1. Clip the hair around the lesion with electric clippers (never scissors — the skin underneath is fragile and you will cut it). Aim for at least a half-inch margin of bare skin around the wound. This exposes the lesion to air, which is half the battle.
  2. Gently clean the area with lukewarm water and a chlorhexidine-based dog skin wash, or a saline solution. Pat dry thoroughly — moisture is the enemy.
  3. Apply a vet-recommended topical such as a chlorhexidine spray, hydrocortisone-based dog product, or a specialized wound-care product like Vetericyn. Avoid human creams unless cleared by your vet — many contain ingredients toxic if licked.
  4. Block the licking. An Elizabethan collar (the cone) or a soft inflatable collar is non-negotiable. A hot spot will not heal if your dog can reach it. Period.
  5. Repeat cleaning two to three times per day until the lesion is dry, scabbed, and noticeably smaller — usually three to seven days.

Call your vet immediately if the lesion is larger than a silver dollar, has thick yellow or green discharge, smells strongly, your dog has a fever or seems lethargic, you see multiple hot spots at once, or there is no clear improvement after 48 hours of home care. These cases typically need oral antibiotics, prescription anti-inflammatories, and sometimes a vet-administered clip-and-clean under sedation.

Prevention: Keeping That Double Coat Hot-Spot Free

Once a Golden has had one hot spot, they are statistically more likely to have another. Prevention comes down to managing the three big risk factors — moisture, mats, and itch.

  • Dry thoroughly after every swim or bath. Towel first, then a high-velocity dog dryer or low-heat blow dryer to push water out of the undercoat. Pay special attention to the chest, armpits, behind the ears, and the rump.
  • Brush at least twice a week — every day during shedding season. Use an undercoat rake to break up compacted fur. Mats trap moisture and skin bacteria invisibly.
  • Stay aggressive with year-round flea prevention. Don't skip months in winter. A single bite can ruin a week.
  • Clean ears weekly with a vet-approved ear cleaner. An itchy ear is the #1 cause of facial hot spots in Goldens.
  • Add omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) to your dog's diet. Studies show consistent omega-3 supplementation reduces inflammatory skin conditions in atopic dogs.
  • Don't over-bathe. Goldens generally need a bath every 4–8 weeks. Too frequent bathing strips skin oils and worsens dryness.

Long-Term Strategy: Address the Root Cause

If your Golden gets more than two hot spots a year, stop treating only the surface and dig into the underlying trigger. A veterinary dermatologist can run intradermal allergy testing or serum allergy panels, identify whether the issue is food-driven or environmental, and discuss long-term tools like Apoquel, Cytopoint injections, or immunotherapy — all of which dramatically reduce the itch-scratch cycle that fuels recurrent hot spots. Plenty of Goldens go from "hot spot every other month" to "none in two years" once the allergy puzzle is solved.

Hot spots are stressful for your Golden and heart-stopping the first time you see one — but they are manageable. Daily hands-on grooming, thorough drying, prompt allergy management, and a quick at-home protocol when one does appear will keep your dog's skin healthy and that famous golden coat at its best.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take a hot spot to heal on a Golden Retriever?

With proper at-home care — clipping, cleaning, topical treatment, and a cone — most small hot spots show major improvement in 3 to 5 days and are fully healed within 7 to 10 days. Vet-treated hot spots with antibiotics typically heal in 7 to 14 days. If you don't see improvement within 48 hours of starting treatment, escalate to your vet.

Are hot spots contagious to other dogs or to people?

No. Hot spots are not contagious. They are the result of your individual dog's skin reacting to a trigger combined with self-trauma — they're not caused by a transmissible bacteria, fungus, or parasite. You can safely keep your other pets in their normal routine, but it's still smart to wash your hands after applying topical treatments.

Can I use Neosporin or human hydrocortisone cream on a hot spot?

Generally no. Many human ointments contain ingredients (like neomycin or zinc oxide) that are mildly toxic if licked, and dogs will lick. Stick to dog-specific products: chlorhexidine sprays, dog-formulated hydrocortisone sprays, or veterinary wound-care products like Vetericyn. When in doubt, call your vet's tech line — most will recommend an over-the-counter dog product to start with.

My Golden gets hot spots every summer — what should I do?

Seasonal recurrence is a strong sign of either a flea allergy or pollen-related atopic dermatitis. Tighten flea prevention to year-round and book a veterinary dermatology appointment. A short course of Cytopoint injections during high-pollen months, or a daily Apoquel regimen, can stop the itch-scratch cycle before it even starts. Also consider rinsing your dog with cool water after every outdoor session to wash off pollens and grass allergens.

Should I shave my Golden Retriever to prevent hot spots?

No — please don't. A Golden's double coat actually protects the skin and regulates temperature; shaving disrupts coat regrowth, can cause permanent texture damage, and removes the sun barrier that protects against sunburn. The right answer is regular brushing with an undercoat rake, thorough drying after swims, and trimming feathering on the legs and rear if it tends to mat. The coat is not the enemy — trapped moisture is.
