Golden Retriever Ear Care: How to Clean, Prevent Infections & Spot Trouble Early

April 2026 · 7 min read · Golden Paws

Golden Retrievers are wonderful in almost every way — but their gorgeous floppy ears come with a catch. Those soft, hanging ear flaps create the perfect warm, moist environment for bacteria and yeast to thrive. The good news? With a simple, consistent ear-care routine, you can keep your Golden's ears healthy, comfortable, and infection-free for life.

Why Golden Retrievers Are So Prone to Ear Problems

If you've ever wondered why your Golden seems to shake their head more than other dogs, their anatomy is largely to blame. A dog's ear canal is shaped like an "L" — moisture, wax, and debris naturally collect in that lower curve, where air circulation is limited. In Golden Retrievers, a heavy ear flap drapes directly over that opening, trapping heat and humidity inside like a lid on a pot.

Add to this the breed's love of water — these dogs were literally bred to retrieve waterfowl from lakes and rivers — and you have a recipe for frequent ear trouble. Every swim or bath sends water straight into that L-shaped canal, where it lingers long after the towel comes out. Veterinarians rank ear infections among the most common reasons Golden Retriever owners visit the clinic.

There's another layer too: Golden Retrievers have a genetic predisposition to environmental and food allergies, as well as thyroid disease, both of which inflame the skin lining the ear canal and make infections far more likely. If your Golden develops recurrent ear infections despite good hygiene, it's worth discussing allergy testing with your vet.

Warning Signs of an Ear Infection

Catching an infection early makes treatment faster, cheaper, and much less uncomfortable for your dog. Learn to recognize these red flags:

  • Head shaking or tilting — one of the first behavioral signs something is wrong
  • Scratching at the ear — with a paw, or rubbing the side of the head along the floor or furniture
  • Redness or swelling inside the ear canal or on the flap itself
  • Discharge — a dark brown, yellowish, or black waxy buildup that goes beyond normal earwax
  • Odor — a musty, yeasty, or distinctly "off" smell from the ear
  • Whimpering when the ear is touched — a sign of pain or tenderness
  • Loss of balance or hearing in more severe or chronic cases

If you notice any of these signs, schedule a vet visit rather than attempting home treatment. Your vet will examine the ear with an otoscope, may take a swab for cytology, and will prescribe the appropriate topical medication — typically a combination of antibiotic, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory agents tailored to whatever is causing the infection.

How to Clean Your Golden Retriever's Ears: Step by Step

Regular at-home cleaning is the single most effective thing you can do to prevent infections. Here's how to do it safely and effectively:

  1. Gather your supplies. You'll need a vet-approved ear cleaner (look for one that contains a drying agent), cotton balls or gauze pads, and high-value treats. Never use cotton swabs — they can push debris deeper into the canal and risk damaging the eardrum.
  2. Choose the right time. Pick a calm moment, not right before a walk when your dog is excitable. After bath time or a swim is ideal since you're already in "maintenance mode."
  3. Lift the ear flap and inspect. Look for any unusual redness, swelling, or heavy discharge before you begin. If anything looks alarming, call your vet instead of proceeding.
  4. Fill the canal with cleaner. Hold the ear flap back and upright, then gently squeeze enough cleaner to fill the entire ear canal — you'll often hear a slight squelching sound. This is normal.
  5. Massage the base of the ear. With the flap still held up, use your fingers to gently massage the cartilage at the base of the ear (where it meets the head) for 20–30 seconds. This loosens debris and works the solution deep into the canal. You may hear a wet, sloshing sound — that's exactly what you want.
  6. Let your dog shake. Step back and let them shake their head. This brings loosened debris up and out of the canal naturally.
  7. Wipe the outer canal. Use a cotton ball or gauze to gently wipe away the debris that has worked its way up. Only clean what you can see — never probe deeper than your first knuckle.
  8. Reward generously. Finish with plenty of praise and a treat. The more positive the experience, the easier future cleanings will be.

How Often Should You Clean Your Golden's Ears?

Most Golden Retrievers do well with ear cleaning every two to four weeks as a baseline routine. However, two situations always call for an immediate cleaning regardless of schedule:

  • After every swim. Water in the canal is the leading trigger for "swimmer's ear" (otitis externa) in water-loving dogs. Dry the outer ear with a soft towel after swimming, then apply cleaner to flush out any moisture.
  • After every bath. Even carefully rinsed ears can trap water. Make ear cleaning part of your post-bath routine.

One important caution: more is not better. Over-cleaning can strip the ear of its natural protective wax and actually irritate the canal, making infections more likely rather than less. If your vet has prescribed a specific schedule, stick to it. And if you notice debris lodged very deep in the ear canal — material you can't reach with a cotton ball — have your vet flush it professionally rather than digging around at home.

Choosing the Right Ear Cleaning Products

Not all ear cleaners are created equal. Here's what to look for when selecting a product for your Golden:

  • Vet-formulated solutions are the safest choice. Ask your veterinarian for a recommendation — they may have a preference based on your dog's specific history.
  • Look for a cleaner that contains a drying agent (such as isopropyl alcohol or boric acid) to help evaporate residual moisture after cleaning, especially if your dog swims frequently.
  • Avoid anything containing hydrogen peroxide or vinegar unless your vet specifically recommends it — these can irritate already-sensitive ear tissue.
  • Skip the cotton swabs entirely. This bears repeating: swabs push debris downward and can puncture the eardrum if your dog moves suddenly.

For dogs with recurring infections, your vet might suggest an ear-drying powder designed to keep the canal dry between cleanings. Some owners of very hairy-eared Goldens also ask their groomer to gently pluck or trim excess hair at the canal opening to improve airflow — though this is a personal choice and opinions vary among professionals.

Building a Prevention Routine That Works

The best ear infection is the one that never happens. Beyond regular cleaning, these habits make a meaningful difference:

  • Dry thoroughly after water exposure. Gently fold the ear flap over the opening and pat — don't rub — with a soft towel. Some owners use a low-heat hair dryer held at a safe distance for a few seconds.
  • Include the ears in every grooming session. Even if you're not doing a full cleaning, give the ear a quick visual check and a sniff. You'll get familiar with your dog's normal baseline and notice changes sooner.
  • Address allergies proactively. If your Golden has seasonal or food allergies, work with your vet to manage them. Uncontrolled allergies are among the most common reasons dogs develop chronic ear infections.
  • Keep regular vet check-ups. Annual wellness exams include an ear inspection. Your vet can catch early-stage issues before they become full infections, and they'll alert you if your dog's ears need more frequent attention.

Golden Retrievers are stoic dogs who sometimes endure discomfort without obvious complaint. Building a consistent ear-care habit — cleaning, inspecting, drying — means you're looking after their comfort even when they're too polite to tell you something's wrong.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my Golden Retriever has an ear infection or just dirty ears?

Normal ears may have a small amount of light-brown wax and a mild, earthy smell. An infection typically presents with darker discharge (yellow, brown-black, or bloody), a distinctly foul or yeasty odor, visible redness, and behavioral signs like scratching or head shaking. When in doubt, have your vet take a look — they can confirm the diagnosis with a quick otoscope exam and swab.

Can I use water or baby oil to clean my dog's ears at home?

Neither is recommended. Water leaves moisture behind in the canal, which can worsen the very problem you're trying to prevent. Baby oil can trap debris. Use a veterinarian-approved ear cleaning solution specifically formulated for dogs — these are designed to break down wax and evaporate cleanly without irritating the tissue.

My Golden loves to swim every day. Is that okay for their ears?

Absolutely — swimming is wonderful exercise and most Goldens adore it. The key is post-swim ear care: dry the outer ear thoroughly and apply your vet-recommended ear cleaner after every swim to flush out any trapped water. With a consistent post-swim routine, daily swimmers can have perfectly healthy ears.

Why does my Golden keep getting ear infections even though I clean their ears regularly?

Recurrent infections are often a sign of an underlying issue, most commonly allergies (environmental or food-related) or hypothyroidism — both of which are very common in the breed. The infection itself may be the symptom, not the root cause. Ask your vet about allergy testing or a thyroid panel if this is a recurring pattern in your dog.

At what age should I start cleaning my Golden Retriever puppy's ears?

Start as early as 8–10 weeks old, even if the ears don't need a full cleaning yet. Use a small amount of solution and a gentle touch, and focus more on making it a positive, treat-rewarded experience than on thorough cleaning. Puppies who grow up comfortable with ear handling become adults who cooperate willingly — making every future cleaning much easier for both of you.
