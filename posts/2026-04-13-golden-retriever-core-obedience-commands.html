The 5 Core Obedience Commands Every Golden Retriever Should Know

April 2026 · 7 min read · Golden Paws

Golden Retrievers are famously eager to please — they practically light up when they sense a training session coming. But that enthusiasm needs direction. Mastering five foundational commands (sit, down, stay, come, and leave it) gives your golden the structure they crave, keeps them safe, and lays the groundwork for a lifetime of joyful cooperation between dog and owner.

Why Core Commands Matter More Than Tricks

It's tempting to skip ahead to the fun stuff — shaking paws, rolling over, bringing the newspaper. But the five core obedience commands aren't just party tricks. They are practical, potentially life-saving tools woven into everyday life. A solid "stay" keeps your golden from bolting out an open door. A reliable "come" can pull them back from a dangerous situation. "Leave it" may one day prevent them from swallowing something toxic on a walk.

Golden Retrievers consistently rank among the most trainable dog breeds, and according to the American Kennel Club (AKC), they thrive on the mental stimulation that structured training provides. Puppies as young as eight weeks old can begin learning basic cues — and the sooner you start, the faster good habits form. That said, adult goldens are equally capable learners; it really is never too late to teach an old dog a new command.

The golden rule of golden retriever training: keep sessions short, upbeat, and reward-based. Science-backed positive reinforcement — rewarding desired behaviors with treats, praise, or play — is not only the most effective approach, it also builds the trust and bond that makes goldens such extraordinary companions.

Command #1: Sit — Your Foundation Stone

"Sit" is almost always the first command taught, and for good reason. It's easy to capture, it's a natural resting position for dogs, and it becomes an invaluable default behavior — a polite way for your golden to say "I'm ready and paying attention."

How to teach it: Hold a small, high-value treat close to your dog's nose, then slowly arc your hand up and back over their head. As their nose follows the treat upward, their hindquarters naturally drop toward the floor. The moment their bottom touches the ground, say "Yes!" (or click if you use a clicker) and deliver the treat. Once they're reliably doing the motion, introduce the word "sit" just before they fold into position.

Aim for three to five minute sessions, two or three times per day. The AKC recommends keeping total daily training time to no more than 15 minutes for puppies — their concentration spans are short, and ending on a win is always better than grinding through fatigue. Goldens learn fastest when they're hungry enough to be motivated but not so hungry they can't focus.

Command #2: Down — The Calm That Carries

While "sit" is great for short pauses, "down" teaches your golden to truly settle — something you'll appreciate when guests arrive, during restaurant patio visits, or at the vet. It is slightly more physically demanding than sit, which is why many goldens initially resist it; being low to the ground can feel vulnerable to some dogs.

How to teach it: Ask your dog to sit. Hold a treat at their nose, then slowly lower your hand straight down toward the ground between their front paws. As their nose follows the treat down, their elbows should start to fold. The moment their chest and elbows touch the floor, mark and reward. If they pop back up, calmly reset and try again. Some goldens respond well to luring the treat slightly forward so they have to stretch into the "down" position.

Be patient — "down" often takes a few more sessions than "sit." Keep the mood light and celebrate every partial success. A golden who gets 80% of the way there today will usually nail it tomorrow.

Command #3: Stay — Teaching Impulse Control

Stay is where impulse control begins. It teaches your golden that sometimes the best thing they can do is... nothing. This is counterintuitive for a breed that's wired to be constantly engaged, so building stay in small increments is key.

How to teach it: Ask your dog to sit or lie down. Show your open palm toward them like a stop sign and say "stay" in a calm, even tone. Wait just one or two seconds, then return to them, reward, and release with a word like "OK" or "free." Gradually increase the three Ds: Duration (how long they hold it), Distance (how far you move away), and Distraction (what's happening around them). Only increase one D at a time — trying to extend all three simultaneously is the most common mistake owners make.

A critical nuance: always return to your dog to reward the stay, rather than calling them to you. If you release them toward you, they may learn that "come" is the reward and start breaking the stay in anticipation. Keeping these two commands distinct early on pays dividends later.

Command #4: Come — The Lifesaving Recall

A bombproof recall — your golden dashing to you happily no matter what — is arguably the most important command on this list. It can literally save their life. Yet it's also one of the most frequently undermined commands, usually because owners accidentally make "come" unpleasant (calling the dog only when playtime is over, when a bath is coming, or when they're in trouble).

How to teach it: Make "come" the best thing that ever happens to your golden. Start indoors or in a fenced yard with minimal distractions. Crouch down, open your arms wide, say "[Name], come!" in a happy, enthusiastic voice, and when they arrive, throw a party — treats, praise, petting. Never scold a dog when they come to you, even if it took them a frustratingly long time. To them, the sequence is: "I came → I got in trouble." Gradually practice in more distracting environments: the backyard, the park, around other dogs.

Many trainers recommend a long training lead (15–30 feet) during the distraction-proofing phase. It keeps your golden safe while letting you practice real-world recalls without risking them running off.

Command #5: Leave It — The Guardian Command

Golden Retrievers explore the world mouth-first. On any given walk, yours might try to investigate a chicken bone, a dead bird, a dropped pill, or someone's discarded sandwich. "Leave it" is the command that tells them: ignore that thing entirely, look at me instead.

How to teach it: Place a low-value treat in your closed fist and present it to your dog. They'll sniff, lick, and paw at your hand. The moment they pull back or look away — even for a split second — say "yes" and reward them with a different, higher-value treat from your other hand. The dog learns that leaving the thing in front of them produces something even better. Gradually progress to treats on the floor with your foot ready to cover them, then to real-world scenarios on leash.

The key distinction: "leave it" means don't touch it at all, ever. It's different from "drop it" (release what's in your mouth). Teaching both is worthwhile, but keep the cues completely separate.

Putting It All Together: Training Tips for Golden Retriever Owners

Even knowing the mechanics of each command, a few broader principles will make or break your training success:

  • Consistency is everything. Every member of the household should use the same cue words and hand signals. A golden who hears "sit," "sit down," "sit!" and "SIT!" as four different commands will understandably get confused.
  • End on a win. If your dog is struggling with a new skill, drop back to something they know well, succeed at it, reward generously, and call it a session. This keeps training associated with success rather than frustration.
  • Vary the rewards. High-value treats (small pieces of chicken, cheese, or commercial training treats) are best for teaching new behaviors. Once a behavior is solid, you can intermittently reward with praise, a game of tug, or a brief play session — variable reinforcement actually makes learned behaviors more durable.
  • Generalize the learning. A golden who sits perfectly in the kitchen may not understand that "sit" also means sit at the park, at the pet store, or in a friend's backyard. Practice each command in a dozen different environments to build a truly reliable dog.
  • Consider a group class. The AKC recommends starting structured classes as early as 7–8 weeks for socialization. Beyond socialization, a good trainer can spot mechanical errors in your technique that are invisible to you — and the group setting provides built-in distractions for proofing.

Most golden retriever owners find that investing consistent effort in these five commands during the first six months transforms an enthusiastic, bouncy puppy into an attentive, joyful companion. The commands become a shared language — and that language is the foundation of everything else you'll ever want to do together.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: At what age should I start training my golden retriever?

You can begin the moment your puppy comes home, typically around 8 weeks old. Short, positive sessions of 3–5 minutes are ideal for young puppies. The window between 6 and 16 weeks is particularly important for learning and retention, but goldens of any age — including adults and seniors — can successfully learn all five core commands with consistent positive reinforcement.

Q: My golden keeps breaking the stay to follow me. What am I doing wrong?

Almost always, this means you've increased the distance or duration too quickly. Go back to a one- or two-second stay right beside your dog, reward reliably, and build from there. It also helps to make your return to the dog very calm and deliberate — excited movement as you walk back can inadvertently trigger them to break position and come to you.

Q: How long does it take to teach a golden retriever these five commands?

Most goldens can learn the basic mechanics of all five commands within two to four weeks of consistent daily practice. However, a truly reliable command — one that holds up with distractions, in new environments, and under excitement — typically takes several months of regular reinforcement. Think of it as an ongoing conversation rather than a one-time lesson.

Q: Should I use treats forever, or will my golden become treat-dependent?

Early in training, treats are essential — they communicate clearly to your dog which behaviors earn rewards. Once a behavior is well-established, you can shift to an intermittent reward schedule (rewarding sometimes, but not every time), which actually makes behaviors more durable. You can also substitute other rewards: enthusiastic praise, a tennis ball toss, or a quick game of tug. The goal is to have a rich enough reward vocabulary that treats are one tool among many.

Q: My rescue golden is two years old. Is it too late to teach these commands?

Absolutely not. Adult goldens are often easier to train than puppies because they have longer attention spans and can settle more readily. Some rescue dogs come with partial training already, which is a bonus. The process may look slightly different — you may need to work through some previously reinforced habits — but with patience and positive reinforcement, adult goldens make remarkable progress. Many owners are surprised at how quickly a two- or three-year-old dog picks up commands once they understand the reward system.
