The Complete Golden Retriever Grooming Guide: Coat, Shedding, Ears & More

March 2026 · 8 min read · Golden Paws

If you live with a Golden Retriever, you already know the ritual: you brush your clothes before leaving the house, you keep a lint roller in every room, and somehow there's still a fine golden haze covering everything you own. That gorgeous, flowing double coat is one of the breed's most iconic features — but it does come with responsibility. The good news is that with the right tools and a consistent routine, keeping your Golden looking (and smelling) their best is entirely manageable, and it can even become a bonding experience you both enjoy.

Understanding the Golden Retriever's Double Coat

Before you can groom your Golden effectively, it helps to understand what you're working with. Golden Retrievers have a double coat made up of two very different layers. The outer coat — sometimes called the topcoat or guard coat — is made of longer, water-repellent hairs that protect your dog from moisture, UV rays, and minor scrapes. Beneath that lies a dense, soft undercoat that acts as natural insulation, keeping your dog warm in winter and — counterintuitively — cooler in summer by trapping air.

This double-coat design is why you should never shave a Golden Retriever. Shaving removes the topcoat that regulates temperature and protects the skin, and the undercoat often grows back unevenly, disrupting the coat's natural function. When it gets hot, your Golden's coat is actually doing them a favour — let it do its job.

The coat also goes through two major shedding cycles each year — typically in spring and autumn — when the undercoat blows out to prepare for the coming season. During these periods (lasting 2–4 weeks each), the amount of fur you'll encounter will increase dramatically. Outside of shedding season, expect moderate, steady shedding year-round.

Brushing: The Most Important Step

Regular brushing is the cornerstone of Golden Retriever grooming. It removes loose hair before it ends up on your sofa, prevents painful mats from forming (especially behind the ears, in the armpits, and around the collar), and distributes the coat's natural oils for a healthy shine.

How often should you brush? Aim for at least 3–4 times per week during normal periods, and daily during shedding season. A 10–15 minute session several times a week is far more effective — and gentler on the coat — than a marathon once-a-month brushing that has to work through weeks of tangles.

For tools, you'll want a small kit:

  • Undercoat rake or deshedding tool — reaches through the topcoat to pull out loose undercoat fur. Use this first, always moving in the direction of hair growth.
  • Slicker brush — great for smoothing the topcoat, removing surface tangles, and finishing the look after the rake.
  • Wide-tooth grooming comb — run this through after brushing to check for any remaining knots, especially in the feathered areas on the legs, chest, and tail.

Always brush before bathing, not after. Bathing a matted coat causes the knots to tighten as they dry, making them much harder to remove.

Bathing Your Golden: How Often and How To

Goldens don't need baths as frequently as you might think. Their water-repellent coat means mud and dirt often brush off once dry, and overbathing strips the natural oils that keep their skin and coat healthy. A bath every 6–8 weeks is typically ideal — though if your dog has a talent for finding the muddiest patch of every park, you may find yourself bathing more often.

When bath time arrives, here's how to do it well:

  1. Brush first to remove loose fur and detangle any knots.
  2. Wet thoroughly — Golden coats are dense, and you need to saturate all the way down to the skin. This often takes longer than you'd expect.
  3. Use a dog-specific shampoo, ideally one formulated for double coats or sensitive skin. Lather well, working from neck to tail.
  4. Rinse twice. Residual shampoo left in the coat can cause skin irritation and a dull, sticky coat.
  5. Apply a conditioner if desired, especially if your dog's coat tends toward dryness. Rinse thoroughly.
  6. Dry completely. A damp undercoat left for hours can create a warm, moist environment that encourages bacterial or yeast growth (sometimes called "hot spots"). Use towels first, then a blow dryer on a low-heat setting if your dog tolerates it — or let them air dry somewhere warm with good airflow.

Ear Care: A Golden's Hidden Vulnerability

Golden Retrievers' floppy ears are adorable — but they also create the perfect environment for moisture, debris, and bacteria to accumulate. The ear flap limits airflow into the ear canal, making Goldens one of the more ear infection-prone breeds. Regular ear care is one of the best things you can do for your dog's long-term comfort and health.

Check your dog's ears at least once a week. You're looking for redness, unusual odour, excessive waxy buildup, or signs of discomfort (frequent head shaking, scratching at the ear). These can all signal a developing infection that warrants a vet visit.

For routine maintenance, use a veterinarian-recommended ear cleaning solution. Squeeze a small amount into the ear canal, gently massage the base of the ear for 20–30 seconds, then let your dog shake their head (step back!) and wipe away the loosened debris with a cotton ball or soft pad. Never use cotton swabs deep in the ear canal, and never use water — it can encourage the very moisture problems you're trying to prevent. Ear cleaning is especially important after swimming, since Goldens tend to love water and waterlogged ears are a fast track to infection.

Nail Trimming: Don't Skip This One

Nail care is one of the most commonly neglected parts of dog grooming — but overgrown nails are genuinely uncomfortable for your dog. When nails grow too long, they push against the ground with every step, altering your dog's gait and putting stress on their joints over time. In severe cases, nails can curl and grow back into the paw pad.

Trim your Golden's nails every 2–3 weeks. If you can hear a clicking sound when they walk on hard floors, the nails are overdue. Use sharp, dog-specific nail clippers or a rotary grinder (many dogs find the vibration less startling than the snap of clippers). The key is to avoid the quick — the blood vessel inside the nail. On Goldens with light or cream-coloured nails, you can often see it as a pinkish line. On darker nails, trim a little at a time and look for a dark dot in the centre of the cut surface as a warning sign you're getting close.

If trimming nails makes you nervous, ask your vet or groomer to walk you through it in person. It's a skill worth learning, and most Goldens adapt to it well when started young and kept positive with treats.

Putting It All Together: Your Grooming Schedule

Consistency is the key to low-effort grooming. Here's a practical routine to keep on your calendar:

  • Every 2–3 days: Brush coat (daily during shedding seasons)
  • Weekly: Check and clean ears; inspect paws and between toes
  • Every 2–3 weeks: Trim nails
  • Every 6–8 weeks: Full bath; trim feathering on ears, paws, and tail if desired
  • Every 4–6 weeks: Visit a professional groomer for a full tidy-up if preferred

Building grooming into a routine from puppyhood makes a world of difference. A puppy who learns that brushing and nail trims mean treats and calm praise becomes an adult dog who stands quietly for the whole process. Even if you adopted an older Golden who wasn't groomed regularly, patience and positive reinforcement can shift their attitude considerably.

Your Golden's coat is a reflection of their overall health, and a consistent grooming routine does far more than keep your home fur-free — it gives you regular, hands-on time to notice any changes in your dog's skin, lumps, or comfort level that might otherwise go undetected. Think of grooming not as a chore, but as a regular check-in with your best friend.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I shave my Golden Retriever in summer to keep them cool?

No — shaving a Golden Retriever is not recommended by vets or breed experts. Their double coat acts as insulation in both directions, keeping heat out as well as in. Shaving removes this natural protection, exposes the skin to sunburn, and can cause the coat to grow back unevenly. Instead, keep up with regular brushing to remove dead undercoat, ensure your dog has access to shade and fresh water, and consider a professional deshedding treatment during peak shedding season.

How do I reduce shedding in a Golden Retriever?

You can't eliminate shedding entirely — it's a natural part of having a double-coated breed — but you can significantly reduce the fur that ends up around your home. Regular brushing (3–4 times per week, daily in shedding season) is the single most effective step. A high-quality diet rich in omega-3 fatty acids also supports skin and coat health, which can reduce excessive shedding. If you notice a sudden increase in shedding outside of the normal seasonal cycles, it's worth mentioning to your vet, as it can sometimes signal a nutritional deficiency, stress, or an underlying health issue.

How do I prevent my Golden from getting ear infections?

Prevention comes down to keeping the ears dry and clean. Check ears weekly, clean them every 1–2 weeks with a vet-approved solution, and always dry them thoroughly after baths or swimming. If your dog swims frequently, make ear cleaning part of your post-swim routine. Avoid inserting anything into the ear canal beyond what you can see. If you notice redness, a bad smell, or your dog scratching at their ears repeatedly, see a vet promptly — caught early, most ear infections clear up quickly with treatment.

My Golden hates having their nails trimmed. What can I do?

Start by desensitising your dog to having their paws handled — pick up and gently hold each paw regularly without any trimming involved, and reward them with treats. Introduce the clippers or grinder gradually, letting them sniff and investigate the tool before using it. Trim just one or two nails per session at first, keeping things short and very positive. Over time, most dogs learn to tolerate — and some even enjoy — nail time. A rotary grinder is often less startling than clippers for anxious dogs. If you're truly stuck, a groomer or vet can help.

Can I groom my Golden at home, or do I need a professional groomer?

Most Golden Retriever owners can handle routine grooming at home with a bit of practice and the right tools. Day-to-day brushing, ear cleaning, and nail trims are all learnable skills. Professional grooming every 6–8 weeks is a great addition if you want a thorough deshedding treatment, a tidy trim of the feathering around the ears, paws, and tail, or just a deep bath with a high-velocity dryer. Many owners find a combination works best — maintaining at home and visiting a groomer seasonally or monthly depending on budget and preference.
